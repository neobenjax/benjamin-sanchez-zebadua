"A waste of time."

That’s a phrase I heard echoed in the hallways more than once at the start of this Work-Integrated Learning (WIL) project. With no hand-holding and ambiguous expectations, half the room was waiting for a script that didn't exist.

But as a Systems Engineer, I know that an undefined environment isn't a bug—it’s an open-source sandbox. I quickly realized that this project is a **mirror**. If you commit zero resources, the output is zero. If you "lock in," the system transforms completely.

## The Autonomy Feature
The wildest part was the absolute freedom. Nobody was holding our hands. We were given the space to manage our own teams, and looking around the room on the final day, the diversity of solutions was striking. There was no "one right way." Every team solved the same problems with a unique flavor of teamwork and technical intuition.

I treated our supervisor’s expertise like a **cheat code**. Accessing direct career advice and unfiltered feedback is high-value data that many leave on the table. For me, WIL wasn't just a project; it was an optimization exercise.

![A collaborative team session illustrating diverse problem-solving approaches](/images/posts/003-intentionality-and-milestones/team-collaboration.jpg)

## The Technical Pivot: Software to Wealth
Moving from the binary logic of a Software Engineer to the nuanced, human complexities of **Financial Planning and Wealth Management** was an architectural shift I underestimated. At the beginning, the transition was so intense it literally gave me migraines. 

But this struggle had a specific signature: **Intentionality**.

We didn't end up in this room by accident. For many of us, it meant moving to Canada, navigating a new culture, operating in a second language, and leaving behind everything familiar. We faced a harsh climate and grinding certifications (IFC, English tests) because we came here to achieve something tangible.

> ### 💡 My Personal Reflection on this Journey (Lambton College - Financial Planning and Wealth Management 2024 - 2026) — One milestone at a time!
Usually, when we reach the end of a journey like this—wrapping up the **Financial Planning and Wealth Management** program and finally finishing this **WIL project**—the first thing we do is thank everyone else. 

But today, I’m going to start by doing something we don't do nearly enough: **I want to thank myself.** I think we all should.

Because getting to this exact moment was not easy, and we deserve to acknowledge our own **grit**. Getting here meant leaving a lot behind. It meant:
* Moving to Canada to start a completely new life away from home.
* Navigating a new culture.
* Operating in a different language.
* Starting over with no family and no friends nearby.
* Facing a harsh climate—even though, honestly, I actually like the winter.
* Grinding to pass an English test and an **IFC certificate**.

And for me, it meant a massive pivot. Going from the **logic of a software engineer** to the **complexities of wealth management**... at the beginning, it was incredibly hard for me—I got so many migraines at the beginning.

> ### "But all of those struggles had one thing in common: Intentionality."

None of us are sitting in this room because someone forced us to be here. We came here to achieve something. And let's be honest—if you are not here by choice, and if you aren't extracting some real, tangible value from this struggle, then it’s simply not worth it. **The effort has to mean something.**

That’s why I want to give a genuine thank you to the professors who actually **gave a damn**. The ones who pushed us and did their absolute best to teach. 
* I’m not talking about the ones who just stood at the front reading off slides.
* Or the ones who assigned work without even knowing what it was.
* Or the ones who barely showed up.

I am thanking the professors who actually **cared enough to teach me something new for my career**. You made the hard parts worth it.

And to all of you... being surrounded by this room—by different people, different cultures, and entirely different points of view—has been one of the greatest experiences of this journey. **That diversity is the true characteristic of Canada**, and it taught me just as much as the textbooks did.

So, we celebrate today. We should. We earned it. 

But tomorrow, the next objective is already waiting. There will be new challenges, new environments, and new walls to break down. We take what we learned, we leave the rest behind, and we **keep moving**. 

**One milestone at a time.**
